# Grant Strategy Guide: Curriculum and Workforce Incubator

Last updated: June 22, 2026

This guide captures the grant positioning that should anchor future Latina Sweat Project grant proposals, award nominations, sponsorship asks, and funder conversations.

## Core Grant Thesis

The strongest grant narrative is not that Latina Sweat Project offers affordable memberships, sliding-scale access, or volunteer exchanges. Those are important access mechanisms, but they are not the most unique part of the model.

The strongest narrative is:

The Latina Sweat Project created a first-of-its-kind, Yoga Alliance-approved 200-hour yoga teacher training curriculum rooted in Latina and Latin American cultural context. That curriculum is the organization's core product and intellectual property. It allows LSP to certify new yoga instructors, incubate its own workforce, create paid employment pathways, and require community service hours that expand free and low-cost wellness access across Chicago.

In grant language, LSP is not just a wellness studio. It is a culturally specific wellness school, workforce incubator, and community health access engine.

## What To Lead With

Lead with these points in most grant proposals:

1. LSP's proprietary curriculum is the innovation.
2. The curriculum is Yoga Alliance-approved and can certify new yoga teachers.
3. The curriculum is culturally specific, rooted in Latina and Latin American context.
4. LSP uses the curriculum to create a workforce pipeline from the community.
5. Certified students and trainees complete service hours, which expands affordable and free programming.
6. The model creates jobs, instructors, classes, community access, and revenue.
7. The school/incubator model is what makes the membership and pricing model possible.

This should usually come before broader language about wellness, community building, mental health, or general access.

## What Not To Over-Lead With

These points are still useful, but they should usually be secondary:

- Sliding-scale or discounted memberships.
- Volunteer exchange for membership.
- General community building.
- General mental health and wellness benefits.
- The studio as a welcoming space.

Why: these themes are meaningful, but funders may see them as common across many nonprofits, studios, or community programs. The curriculum and workforce incubator are more concrete, more defensible, and more fundable.

Better framing:

- Do not say the sliding-scale membership is the innovation.
- Say the curriculum and incubator make the affordable pricing model possible.
- Do not say the volunteer model is unique by itself.
- Say service hours are part of a structured training and workforce pipeline that produces community access while preparing new instructors.

## Strategic Positioning

### Primary Identity

The Latina Sweat Project is a Latina-founded nonprofit wellness school and studio that trains, certifies, employs, and supports BIPOC yoga instructors while expanding affordable wellness access in Chicago.

### Primary Product

The product is the Latina Sweat 200-hour teacher training curriculum: a proprietary, culturally specific, Yoga Alliance-approved curriculum rooted in Latina and Latin American lived experience, history, and community wellness.

### Primary Competitive Advantage

LSP's competitive advantage is its ability to create its own instructor workforce from the community it serves.

This means LSP is not dependent only on the existing yoga labor market, which often lacks cultural representation and can be cost-prohibitive. LSP can train new instructors, certify them, place them into paid teaching roles, and connect their training to community service.

### Secondary Competitive Advantage

LSP's service-hour model turns teacher training into community benefit. Students and newly certified instructors contribute teaching, volunteer, and service hours, which helps LSP keep classes affordable while creating real-world teaching experience and a pathway into paid work.

## Strongest Grant Narrative

Use this version when space allows:

The Latina Sweat Project is a Latina-founded nonprofit wellness school and studio in Chicago. Our core innovation is a first-of-its-kind, Yoga Alliance-approved 200-hour teacher training curriculum rooted in Latina and Latin American cultural context. This curriculum is not an add-on to our studio model; it is the product that powers our impact. It allows us to certify new yoga instructors from the communities most often excluded from the wellness industry, create paid teaching pathways, and build our own culturally representative workforce.

Through this school and incubator model, LSP is able to keep wellness access affordable. Teacher trainees and certified instructors complete structured service hours that support free and low-cost classes, community events, and neighborhood wellness programming. The result is a self-reinforcing model: our curriculum creates instructors, instructors create classes, classes generate revenue and community access, and revenue/funding sustains the next cohort.

Unlike traditional studios that recruit from an existing instructor market, LSP trains and employs its own community-rooted teachers. Unlike general wellness nonprofits, LSP has a credential-conferring education product that creates jobs, revenue, and community health access at the same time.

## 100-Word Grant Answer

Use this for tight application fields:

The Latina Sweat Project's core innovation is a first-of-its-kind, Yoga Alliance-approved 200-hour teacher training curriculum rooted in Latina and Latin American cultural context. This curriculum allows LSP to certify new yoga instructors, incubate its own culturally representative workforce, and create paid teaching pathways for BIPOC community members. Trainees and graduates complete structured service hours that support free and low-cost classes, helping keep wellness accessible while building real teaching experience. The model converts curriculum into jobs, classes, revenue, and community health access, making LSP more than a studio: it is a nonprofit wellness school and workforce incubator.

## Problem Statement

Chicago's wellness industry often excludes Latina, immigrant, BIPOC, and working-class communities through high prices, limited cultural representation, and instructor pipelines that do not reflect the neighborhoods being served. Even when classes are offered at lower cost, the underlying workforce and curriculum often remain disconnected from community identity, language, history, and lived experience. LSP addresses both access and representation by building a culturally specific teacher training curriculum and using it to certify instructors from the community.

## Solution Statement

LSP created a proprietary 200-hour teacher training curriculum rooted in Latina and Latin American context and approved by Yoga Alliance. The curriculum enables LSP to train and certify new yoga instructors, create a workforce pipeline, and connect training to community service. Students and graduates support free and low-cost programming through service hours while gaining experience that can lead to paid teaching roles. This makes the curriculum both an educational product and the engine behind LSP's affordable access model.

## Innovation Statement

LSP's innovation is the combination of culturally specific curriculum, credentialing authority, workforce incubation, and community access. The organization is not simply offering yoga classes at lower prices. It is building a new instructor pipeline through a curriculum that reflects Latina and Latin American communities, then using that pipeline to staff classes, create jobs, and expand free and low-cost wellness access.

## Workforce and Jobs Framing

Use this when a grant values economic mobility, workforce development, or job creation:

LSP functions as a workforce incubator for BIPOC wellness professionals. Through its teacher training school, LSP prepares students to become certified yoga instructors, supports them through service hours and supervised practice, and creates pathways into paid teaching. Across cohorts, LSP is moving toward approximately 100 certified or in-training instructors, with about 60 participants already connected to paid teaching or paid wellness work. This workforce pipeline is central to the model: LSP creates the instructors it needs, and those instructors expand access for the broader community.

## Revenue and Value Framing

Use this when a grant asks about sustainability, earned revenue, or market value:

LSP's curriculum is also a revenue and value generator. Comparable 200-hour yoga teacher trainings often cost $5,000 to $7,000 per student. A 50-person cohort represents approximately $250,000 to $350,000 in training value, with a conservative minimum value of $250,000. As LSP scales toward approximately 100 trained or in-training instructors, the value of the curriculum and workforce pipeline grows substantially. This education product supports earned revenue, grant competitiveness, community access, and long-term sustainability.

## How To Connect Affordability To The Curriculum

Preferred wording:

Our low-cost and free classes are possible because we are not only a studio; we are a school. The teacher training curriculum creates a pipeline of instructors who complete service hours, gain real teaching experience, and eventually move into paid teaching roles. This reduces the cost of delivering classes while preserving quality and cultural alignment.

Avoid:

- Our sliding-scale model is unique.
- Our volunteer exchange is the innovation.
- People volunteer to get memberships, and that is how we keep prices low.

Use instead:

- Our curriculum and service-hour practicum make affordable access possible.
- Our school creates instructors, and our instructors create access.
- Our workforce incubator converts education into community wellness delivery.

## Evidence To Gather Before Future Submissions

Before major grant submissions, update or confirm:

- Exact number of certified instructors by cohort.
- Exact number of active trainees.
- Exact number of paid instructors.
- Exact number of people paid through adjacent roles, such as cleaning, operations, coffee shop/vendor support, or event staffing.
- Exact number of service hours completed by trainees/graduates.
- Number of classes generated by service hours.
- Dollar value of donated/subsidized teacher training seats.
- Whether the curriculum is formally copyrighted or should be described as proprietary/copyright-protected.
- Whether IP ownership should be described as founder-owned, organization-owned, licensed to the organization, or jointly held.
- Current Yoga Alliance approval language.
- Current earned revenue from teacher training, if any.
- Market comparison for 200-hour YTT tuition in Chicago and nationally.

## Proposal Checklist

Before submitting a grant, make sure the application:

- Leads with curriculum as the product.
- Names the Yoga Alliance-approved 200-hour teacher training curriculum.
- Explains why Latina/Latin American cultural context makes the curriculum distinct.
- Describes LSP as a school and workforce incubator.
- Connects certification to job creation and paid teaching pathways.
- Connects service hours to free or low-cost community access.
- Includes curriculum value, not only studio revenue.
- Includes cohort numbers and projected instructor totals.
- Uses wellness and mental health language as outcomes, not the primary innovation.
- Avoids overclaiming sliding-scale membership or volunteer exchange as unique.
- Includes the strongest metrics available from QuickBooks, MarianaTek, Zeffy, and cohort records.
- Separates confirmed facts from projections.

## Notes On Tone

Grant language should sound concrete and outcome-driven. Lead with deliverables:

- We certified this many people.
- We created this many paid teaching pathways.
- We generated this much training value.
- We delivered this many classes.
- We subsidized this much access.
- We built the only curriculum of its kind.

Use broader values language after the measurable model is clear:

- Healing.
- Joy.
- Community.
- Mental health.
- Cultural belonging.
- Wellness equity.

The strongest applications will combine both, but the measurable curriculum/incubator model should come first.
